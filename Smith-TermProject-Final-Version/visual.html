Birds have many characteristics and it can be overwhelming at first. Color is where many people think to start, but this can actually be rather challenging as colors change during the year. Below are some other ideas I have picked up from the National Audubon Website

Groups

For a beginning birder, I suggest learning a few birds in the basic groups we see in Oregon and looking for commonalities and differences between birds in the groups.

  • Swans, ducks, and geese
    • Mallard
    • Canada Goose
    • Cackling Goose
    • Tundra Swan
  • Chickadees and nuthatches
    • Black-capped chickadee
    • Bushtit
    • White-breasted nuthatch
  • Wrens and Kinglets
    • Bewick's wren
    • Ruby-crowned kinglet
  • Bitterns, herons, and egrets
    • Great blue heron
    • Great egret

Size and Shape

Within these groups, features like size, body shape, and beak shape can help. For example, once you see enough wrens, you start to realize they all have long beaks that are either straight or slightly curve down. At first, birders can get them confused with nuthatches, but nuthatches have straight, almost chisel-shaped beaks.

Behavior

Sometimes, even knowing your basic groups and characteristics may not lead to a quick identification. You can also consider the behavior of birds when you are trying to visually identify them.

For example, when you are looking for chickadees in the West, you are often looking for busy, acrobatic birds located in deciduous trees. On the other hand, wrens, while they are busy, are often located in low tree branches and shrubs.
